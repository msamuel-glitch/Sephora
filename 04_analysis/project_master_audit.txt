SECTION 1 — DATA BASICS
================================================================================

What we started with:
- One anonymised transaction dataset covering the full calendar year 2025.
- 399,997 transaction rows across 34 columns.
- Each row represents a single product purchase by a loyalty-card customer.

Key dimensions in the raw data:
- Customer identity (anonymised loyalty card number)
- Transaction details (date, sales in EUR including VAT, discount in EUR, quantity)
- Product details (material code, product category, market segment, brand)
- Store context (store name, city, channel, store type)
- Customer profile (age, gender, loyalty status, customer value ranking)
- First-purchase history (available for 26% of customers only)

Data quality issues identified and resolved:
- 892 exact duplicate rows were permanently removed.
- 5,210 negative-sales rows and 4,979 negative-quantity rows were flagged
  (these represent returns and were retained with anomaly flags).
- 8,008 zero-sales and 5,676 zero-quantity rows were flagged.
- 14,571 rows where the discount exceeded the sale price were flagged.
- 6,168 rows with missing brand names and 6,346 with missing market
  descriptions were filled with "UNKNOWN" after a recovery attempt yielded
  zero matches.
- 11 columns relating to first-purchase history were missing for 74% of rows.
  These were retained for the subset of customers where they were available
  but were not used as mandatory inputs.

After cleaning and applying the eligibility rule (removing flagged anomaly
rows that would distort the affinity signal), the working dataset contained:
- 385,879 eligible rows
- 50,805 unique customers
- 239 unique brands

Time-split design:
- Feature Window:  January to September 2025 (259,023 rows) — used to learn
  customer preferences.
- Target Window:   October to December 2025 (126,856 rows) — used to test
  whether the system correctly predicted what customers would buy next.

This strict time separation ensures that the system never "peeked" at future
purchases when building its recommendations. A formal leakage audit confirmed
every feature was computed exclusively from the January-to-September window.

Sources: schema_report.txt, cleaning_report.txt, feature_engineering_log.txt,
         leakage_audit.txt

SECTION 2 — FEATURE FAMILIES
================================================================================

The system organises customer and brand information into three layers,
each adding a different type of intelligence:

LAYER 1 — Core Affinity Signal (What do customers actually buy together?)
- Basket co-occurrence:   45,446 multi-brand baskets were analysed to find
                          8,357 unique brand pairs. After filtering for
                          statistical reliability (minimum 3 co-purchases),
                          3,998 brand pairs were retained.
- Customer-brand matrix:  A purchase history grid of 50,805 customers
                          × 239 brands was built to capture individual
                          shopping patterns.
- Customer-level features: 19 metrics per customer, including:
  · Explorer index — how willing a customer is to try new brands
    (median: 0.41, meaning most customers sit between loyal and exploratory)
  · Basket density — how many product categories appear in a single visit
  · Brand loyalty concentration — how much spend goes to a single brand

LAYER 2 — Business Value Overlay (What makes a recommendation commercially smart?)
- Generation-aware profiling: Purchase patterns were calculated for each
  generation (Gen Z, Gen Alpha, Gen Y, Gen X, Baby Boomers).
- Market migration scores: Tracking whether customers are trading up from
  mid-range to premium brands (or vice versa) within the January-to-September
  window.
- Cold-start brand classification: 141 brands classified as "Established"
  and 98 as "Emerging" (fewer than the threshold number of historical
  transactions).
- High-frequency customer identification: 6,188 customers purchasing at
  intervals of 26 days or fewer (top quartile of purchase velocity).
- Brand volatility index: Measuring how stable a brand's customer base is
  over time, based on repurchase counts.

LAYER 3 — Activation & Storytelling (How should Sephora reach each customer?)
- Every customer was assigned a best-fit activation channel:
  · In-Store guidance:      35,650 customers (70.2%)
  · CRM direct outreach:    11,090 customers (21.8%)
  · App/website suggestion:  4,065 customers (8.0%)

Guardrail flags applied to brand pairs:
- 217 pairs flagged for high-price artifacts (e.g., a single expensive item
  inflating the co-occurrence signal).
- 179 pairs flagged for trivial associations (e.g., two brands from the same
  parent company always sold together).

Sources: feature_engineering_log.txt, validation_report.txt

SECTION 3 — CANDIDATE ENGINE
================================================================================

Before any machine learning model runs, the system generates a shortlist of
plausible brand recommendations for each customer. This is the "candidate
engine" — the filter that decides which brands are even worth scoring.

Four candidate routes feed the shortlist:

1. Basket Pair Route
   Brands that frequently appear in the same shopping basket. Only pairs with
   a co-occurrence lift above 1.5× (meaning 50% more likely than random
   chance) are included.

2. Lookalike Route
   Brands purchased by customers with similar shopping profiles, identified
   through a mathematical similarity measure applied to the January-to-
   September brand purchase vectors.

3. Axis-Market Route
   Brands within the same product category and market segment that a customer
   has shown affinity for, but has not yet purchased.

4. Cold-Start Route
   Emerging brands with fewer historical transactions are mapped to
   established brand proxies so that even new brands can receive
   recommendations.

Candidate set sizes vary by generation:
- Gen Alpha customers:   median 59 candidates per customer
- Gen Z customers:       median 48 candidates per customer
- Gen Y customers:       median 41 candidates per customer
- Gen X customers:       median 38 candidates per customer
- Baby Boomers:          median 30 candidates per customer
- Unknown generation:    median 37 candidates per customer

The younger the customer, the wider the candidate set — reflecting their
higher willingness to explore new brands.

High-price artifact guardrail: 262,171 candidate rows triggered the high-
price filter and were excluded before scoring to prevent inflated
recommendations driven by a single expensive purchase.

Sources: candidate_exploration_report.txt, feature_engineering_log.txt

SECTION 4 — THREE MODELS TESTED
================================================================================

Three recommendation approaches were trained and tested head-to-head on the
October-to-December holdout window.

MODEL 1: Popularity Baseline
- Logic:     Recommend the most-purchased brands overall, regardless of
             individual customer preferences.
- Top-3 accuracy:  4.02%
- Purpose:   Benchmark — any intelligent system must beat this.

MODEL 2: Business Hybrid Engine
- Logic:     A rule-based system combining basket affinity, business priority
             weights, and activation channel fit. Designed for commercial
             control and diversity of recommendations.
- Top-3 accuracy:  0.52%
- Top-5 ranking quality: 0.0154
- Why it scores lower: It optimises for high-quality activation routing and
  recommendation diversity, not pure ranking of purchase volume. It
  intentionally surfaces niche and emerging brands that volume-based models
  would ignore.

MODEL 3: Machine Learning Model (the trained prediction engine)
- Logic:     A gradient-based decision tree model trained on all customer and
             brand features to predict which brands a customer will purchase
             in the holdout window.
- Top-3 accuracy:  5.26%  ← highest predictive accuracy
- Top-5 ranking quality: 0.1453
- This model excels at fine-grained predictive ranking, especially for
  customers with high explorer tendencies.

FINAL DECISION — Ensemble Router:
The production system uses neither model alone. Instead, it routes customers
to the best-performing model for their profile:
- Customers with explorer index > 0.4 → Machine Learning Model
- Core loyalist customers → Business Hybrid Engine
- Combined top-3 accuracy: 1.35% (95% confidence interval: 1.29% to 1.41%)

The top 20 most influential features driving the machine learning model:
1.  Total brand purchase quantity (importance: 0.545)
2.  Total brand customer count (importance: 0.537)
3.  Total brand spend in EUR (importance: 0.460)
4.  Total brand transaction count (importance: 0.427)
5.  Number of distinct brands purchased (importance: 0.179)
6.  Customer value ranking segment (importance: 0.179)
7.  Loyalty programme status (importance: 0.165)
8.  Gen Y brand appeal score (importance: 0.121)
9.  Basket pair presence flag (importance: 0.106)
10. Recruitment channel (importance: 0.091)
11. Purchase velocity score (importance: 0.077)
12. Gen Z brand appeal score (importance: 0.072)
13. Gen Alpha brand appeal score (importance: 0.067)
14. Customer value trajectory (importance: 0.066)
15. Launch readiness score (importance: 0.066)
16. Average basket size in EUR (importance: 0.062)
17. Total transaction count (importance: 0.058)
18. Explorer index (importance: 0.054)
19. Baby Boomer brand appeal score (importance: 0.050)
20. App recommendation affinity proxy (importance: 0.046)

SECTION 5 — FIVE KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

FINDING 1: The Explorer Effect
Customers with an explorer index above the median (0.41) responded
significantly better to machine learning recommendations than to popularity-
based ones. The machine learning model's advantage over the baseline was
driven almost entirely by these open-to-discovery customers.
- ML-only winner mean explorer index: 0.582
- Hybrid-only winner mean explorer index: 0.605
This confirms that the system adds the most value where customers are already
inclined to try something new.

FINDING 2: Generation Shapes Brand Affinity
Baby Boomers show a structurally different purchase profile:
- Fragrance-led (32.2% of spend) rather than makeup-led.
- 65.2% of their brand spend goes to mid-range (Selective) brands vs. only
  22.8% to premium (Exclusive).
- Explorer index: median 0.64, the lowest of all generations.
- The machine learning model's accuracy for Boomers in the Haircare category
  dropped to 0.81%, well below the overall average. This triggered the
  generation-mismatch suppression rule (see Section 6).

FINDING 3: High-Frequency Customers Are a CRM Goldmine
6,188 customers purchase at intervals of 26 days or fewer.
- 90.0% of all high-frequency customers are captured by the CRM direct
  outreach activation channel.
- 50.2% of the CRM outreach segment are high-frequency customers.
- These customers represent the highest-velocity segment for brand discovery
  campaigns.

FINDING 4: The Balanced Scenario Wins
Three commercial weighting scenarios were tested against 20,181 customers
with active target-window purchases:
- Margin-first:     3.10% customer adoption rate (626 customers converted)
- Balanced:         3.27% customer adoption rate (659 customers converted) ←
- Discovery-first:  2.89% customer adoption rate (583 customers converted)
The Balanced scenario — an equal weighting of margin and discovery signals —
produced the highest conversion rate, confirming that neither pure profit-
chasing nor pure novelty-seeking maximises real-world adoption.

FINDING 5: Incremental Revenue Is Measurable
Customers who adopted a recommended brand spent an average of EUR 84.02 in
the October-to-December window, compared to EUR 72.53 for non-adopters.
- Incremental delta: EUR 11.49 per converted customer.
- Total scaled addressable opportunity: EUR 583,570 across the eligible
  customer base.
This provides the first quantifiable business case for deploying the
recommendation engine.

Sources: 06_model_bakeoff_results.txt, step3_4_report.txt,
         candidate_exploration_report.txt

SECTION 6 — SUPPRESSION LAYER (The Protection Filter)
================================================================================

The protection filter blocks unsuitable recommendations from reaching high-
value customers. It operates on three axes:

AXIS A — Business Guardrails
15 brand-pair combinations were identified as commercially risky:
- Margin-dilutive pairings (e.g., recommending a discount brand to a premium
  customer).
- Luxury/mid-range planogram conflicts (e.g., pairing AVEDA with CHANEL).
These 15 pairs are permanently blocked across all customer segments.

AXIS B — Generation Mismatch (Baby Boomer Protection)
The system automatically suppresses recommendations in any product category
where Baby Boomer response rates fall below a safety threshold.
- Threshold: response rate below 0.0204 (calculated as the overall mean
  minus one standard deviation).
- Result: 14 Haircare brands suppressed for Baby Boomer customers.
  Examples include BRIOGEO, BUMBLE & BUMBLE, FABLE & MANE, KERASTASE
  sub-brands, OLAPLEX, and VIRTUE.
- Rationale: Recommending trendy haircare brands to Baby Boomers — who
  overwhelmingly prefer fragrance and skincare — would erode trust without
  generating conversions.

AXIS C — High-Value Customer False Positive Protection
The most critical axis. It identifies customers who:
  (a) hold a high customer value ranking (segments 1-3),
  (b) have elite loyalty status (levels 3-4),
  (c) received a top-3 recommendation from the model, AND
  (d) did not purchase the recommended brand in the holdout window, AND
  (e) the recommendation violated at least one business guardrail.

Results:
- Unique customers affected: 6,995
- Relationship equity at risk: EUR 491,476
  (calculated from each customer's individual average order value over the
   January-to-September feature window)
- Protection coverage rate for high-value customers: 53.65%

These 6,995 customers are placed on a "do not recommend" list for the
flagged brand pairs. They are NOT excluded from all recommendations — only
from the specific pairings where the system simultaneously produced a low-
confidence prediction AND violated a business guardrail.

Sources: suppression_logic.txt, do_not_recommend_layer.csv

SECTION 8 — VALIDATION & A/B TEST DESIGN
================================================================================

VALIDATION SUMMARY:
- Time-split integrity: CONFIRMED. All features computed from January-to-
  September data only. The October-to-December window was a pure out-of-time
  holdout.
- Leakage audit: CLEAN. Every feature was traced back to its source code and
  confirmed to use only feature-window data. No suspect or confirmed leakage
  was found.
- One blueprint feature ("repeat_potential_proxy") was documented in the
  original design but never implemented. It carries zero leakage risk.

A/B TEST DESIGN:
The system proposes a two-arm A/B test for production deployment:

Treatment Arm (High-Confidence Activation):
- Scope:     Top 10% of machine learning probability scores.
- Threshold: Predicted probability >= 0.021857.
- Logic:     Direct recommendation with no suppression overhead.
- Objective: Measure incremental revenue capture against the control group.

Control Arm (Suppression-Protected):
- Scope:     6,995 high-value customers identified by the protection filter.
- Logic:     Standard merchandising with no model-driven recommendations.
- Objective: Confirm that suppressing guardrail-violating recommendations
             preserves customer relationship equity.

Success Metrics:
- Primary:   Incremental conversion rate (treatment vs. control).
- Secondary: Average basket size change (target: > EUR 11.49 incremental
             delta observed in the holdout test).
- Guardrail: Churn rate in the suppressed segment must not exceed the
             baseline churn rate for equivalent loyalty tiers.

Deployment Roadmap:
1. PILOT — Launch A/B test on the 6,995 high-value customers using the
   protection filter as the split criterion.
2. MARGIN CHECK — Integrate cost-of-goods data to convert "relationship
   equity at risk" into "margin at risk" for CFO-level approval.
3. API INTEGRATION — Embed the suppression check into the real-time scoring
   payload for CRM and in-store recommendation triggers.

Sources: leakage_audit.txt, suppression_logic.txt
