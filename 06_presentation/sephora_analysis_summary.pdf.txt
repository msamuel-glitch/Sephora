Why this analysis?
The starting point was a transactional dataset of 399,997 purchase lines from Sephora France, covering 64,469 unique loyalty
cardholders across 2024-2025. The business question was simple but ambitious: for any given customer, predict what they will
buy next — what product, from which brand, through which channel, and at what value. Rather than building a single
black-box model, we chose a step-by-step approach that keeps every insight explainable and directly actionable for CRM and paid
media teams. The five steps below each answer a distinct question and build on the previous one.
1
Revenue & Brand Performance by Generation
What we did. After filtering returns and customers with unknown generation labels, we retained 267,153 purchase lines across four
generations. We aggregated total revenue, average basket and transaction count per generation, then ranked brands by revenue
within each generation group.
Fig 1. Total revenue and average basket per generation.
Fig 2. Top 5 brands by generation — total revenue (k€).
Key findings.
Gen Z generates the highest total revenue (€2.73M) but Baby Boomers have the highest average basket (€46.11 vs €28-32 for other
generations). The same purchase behaviour produces 35-60% more value when performed by an older customer.

Baby Boomers are the only generation where SEPHORA is not the top brand — DIOR leads at 12.4% market share, followed by
GUERLAIN and CHANEL. Their top 10 is composed exclusively of luxury heritage houses (Hermès, Sisley, Clarins, Lancôme).
Gen A shows the most digital-native brand mix: HUDA BEAUTY ranks #2 (6.8%) ahead of DIOR (5.1%), and Charlotte Tilbury enters
the top 5. These are brands built on social media virality rather than heritage prestige.
SEPHORA collection acts as a universal bridge across all generations — it is the #1 brand in every generation except Baby Boomers,
functioning as an entry-level or rebound product between premium brand purchases.
2
Product Axis Analysis by Generation & Gender
What we did. We broke down revenue, transaction count and average basket by product axis (MAKE UP, FRAGRANCE,
SKINCARE, HAIRCARE) for each generation, then introduced gender as an additional dimension. Gender encodes as 1 = male, 2 =
female in the dataset. With only 4,440 male customers (6% of the base) versus 38,577 female, the male segment is small but
extremely high-value per transaction.
Fig 3. Revenue by product axis and generation (k€).
Fig 4. FRAGRANCE average basket by generation and gender (€).
Key findings.
FRAGRANCE is the most profitable axis in every single generation, with an average basket 3-4x higher than MAKE UP. Yet it
represents only 12% of Gen A purchases — a massive untapped opportunity. Every customer converted to FRAGRANCE triples the
transaction value.
Male customers spend €52.44 on average per visit — the highest in the dataset — driven by FRAGRANCE which accounts for 69% of
male revenue (€95.98 avg basket). Despite representing only 6% of customers, they are the most profitable per transaction.
Female customers dominate volume (251,874 transactions) but concentrate on MAKE UP (€24.25 avg basket). The gap between
FRAGRANCE (€83) and MAKE UP (€24) for women represents the single largest value-creation opportunity in the dataset.
The male path to Sephora is direct: men enter via FRAGRANCE and stay there. The female path is indirect: women enter via MAKE
UP, cross to SKINCARE, and only gradually discover FRAGRANCE. Understanding this difference fundamentally changes how to
design acquisition campaigns for each gender.
3
Customer Loyalty & Retention by Generation

What we did. We aggregated all purchases at the customer level and computed retention rate (% of customers with 2 or more
distinct ticket visits), average number of tickets, average lifespan (days between first and last purchase), and brand diversity (number
of distinct brands purchased). This step was critical to understand not just who buys, but who comes back — and why the most loyal
customers are not always the most profitable per visit.

Key findings.
Gen A is the most loyal generation (60.7%) despite having the lowest average basket (€28). They visit more often, explore more
brands (3.60 on average), and stay engaged longer (120 days). This is a "discovery" profile — Sephora is their exploration
playground.
Baby Boomers have the lowest retention (50.2%) but spend the most per visit (€46). Critically, 3,176 Boomer customers visited only
once. Each of these represents an immediate reactivation opportunity with a guaranteed €46+ basket — the highest ROI reactivation
segment in the dataset.
Brand diversity correlates strongly with retention: Gen A explores 3.60 brands, Boomers only 2.50. The insight is counterintuitive —
loyalty is not built by selling more of one brand, but by introducing customers to new ones. Multi-brand exploration drives repeat visits.
4
Transition Rules — What Do Customers Buy Next?
Why this step. Knowing who customers are and what they buy is not enough. The real business value lies in predicting the next
purchase before it happens. We built this by sorting each customer's purchases chronologically and using a shift(-1) operation to pair
each purchase (N) with the following one (N+1). This created 224,212 observed transitions — each one a ground-truth example of
"this profile bought X, then bought Y."
How we turned transitions into rules. Rather than training a single model (which gave only 52% accuracy on axis prediction), we
computed conditional probability tables — for each combination of gender × generation × current axis × current brand × channel, we
calculated: (1) the most likely next axis, (2) the top 3 next brands, (3) the most likely next channel, and (4) the expected next basket
value. Applying a minimum of 20 transitions per group for statistical reliability, we produced 894 actionable behavioural rules.

Key findings.
SEPHORA is the universal rebound brand — it appears as the #1 next brand in 100% of rules. This is not loyalty to the own-brand: it is
a bridge behaviour between two premium brand purchases. Customers use SEPHORA products to fill the gap between two DIOR or
CHANEL buys.
The current brand changes the expected next basket but not the next axis. Whether a Gen Z woman buys SEPHORA (€24) or HUDA
BEAUTY (€30), she still goes back to MAKE UP 70% of the time. Brand and category are two independent levers that must be
optimised separately.
HUDA BEAUTY Gen Z MAKE UP has the strongest same-axis loop (71.4%) of any non-Fragrance rule. These customers are the
most brand-loyal outside FRAGRANCE — ideal for replenishment campaign automation.
Chanel FRAGRANCE Gen Y is perfectly balanced: 38% go back to FRAGRANCE, 38% go to MAKE UP. This is the ideal intervention
moment — a push toward FRAGRANCE consolidates the high-value behaviour, while a MAKE UP push generates a €55 cross-sell.
5
The Targeting Table — 894 Rules, One Row per Profile
Why this final step. The goal was never to produce a model score. It was to produce a targeting table that any CRM or paid media
platform can ingest directly: one rule per row, each row telling the campaign team exactly which customer profile to target, with what
product and brand, through which channel, and at what expected revenue. This table is sortable by expected basket value —
meaning budget can be allocated from the highest-ROI profiles downward.
Fig 6. Top 10 rules by expected next basket (€). All are FRAGRANCE → FRAGRANCE transitions. Blue = male profile, pink = female profile.
Key findings.
The top 10 most valuable rules are all FRAGRANCE → FRAGRANCE — without exception. A customer already in FRAGRANCE
stays in FRAGRANCE with 50-70% probability and generates €86-103 on their next visit. This confirms the strategic priority: protect
FRAGRANCE customers, do not try to convert them to other categories.
6 of the top 10 rules are male profiles despite men being only 6% of the customer base. Male FRAGRANCE buyers are the most
consistently high-value segment. J.P. Gaultier and Chanel Gen Z men generate €90+ expected next basket — comparable to Boomer
women buying heritage prestige.
REMINISCENCE female Boomers top the ranking at €102.87 — a niche brand with exceptional repeat loyalty. These customers never
switch brands and generate the highest expected basket in the entire dataset. A personalised VIP programme with exclusive
REMINISCENCE access would have immediate ROI.

The Lancôme → DIOR male Gen Y rule (€87.83) is the only cross-brand rule in the top 10. It is the most actionable cross-sell in the
dataset: a man who just bought Lancôme FRAGRANCE is statistically the most likely next DIOR buyer. A targeted DIOR campaign
post-Lancôme purchase is a high-confidence, high-value campaign.
