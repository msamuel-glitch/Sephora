# Final Deliverable Governance
## Sephora Case 3 — Brand Affinity Detection

## Purpose
This document defines the governing principles for the final deliverable.  
It does not predict findings, invent recommendations, or pre-write the story before analysis.  
Its role is to ensure that the final deck, website, or hybrid format remains analytically credible, brand-relevant, visually strong, and actionable.

## Core rule
The final deliverable must be led by evidence, not imagination.  
Creative direction is allowed only if it clarifies or strengthens the communication of real findings.  
No narrative, recommendation, visual metaphor, or business claim should be finalized before the data analysis supports it.

## Why this approach
Sephora expects teams to work from business context toward analysis, create relevant KPIs, challenge obvious interpretations, and transform data into actions that create value.  
The final output must therefore emerge from the analysis, not from a pre-written concept.

## Non-negotiable principles

### 1. Evidence before expression
No key conclusion may appear in the final deliverable unless it is supported by analysis.
This includes:
- recommended brands,
- customer archetypes,
- claims of uplift,
- statements about loyalty or value,
- statements about discovery potential,
- statements about what should be activated.

### 2. Actionability over decoration
The final deliverable must not stop at insight.
Each major finding should lead to a possible action, such as:
- CRM targeting,
- app personalization,
- e-merchandising,
- store guidance,
- campaign prioritization.

### 3. Correlation is not enough
Any affinity finding must be examined carefully.
The team must avoid presenting simple co-purchase or high-price effects as if they were strategic discovery insights.
Where possible, the final deliverable should distinguish:
- popularity,
- co-occurrence,
- similarity,
- predicted adoption potential,
- business usefulness.

### 4. Business value must be visible
The final output must connect the analysis to one or more of the following:
- average basket increase,
- customer value increase,
- accelerated brand discovery,
- improved personalization,
- stronger recommendation relevance.

### 5. Creativity must serve clarity
The final design may be artistic, cinematic, interactive, or scroll-based.
However, design must never:
- hide weak analysis,
- replace explanation,
- imply unsupported findings,
- overload the audience with effects.

## What the final deliverable must achieve
The final deliverable should allow a judge or business stakeholder to understand:
1. what business problem Sephora gave us,
2. what analytical path we followed,
3. what the data revealed,
4. why the findings are credible,
5. how Sephora could activate them,
6. why the solution is stronger than a basic approach.

## What the final deliverable must not do
The final deliverable must not:
- invent outcomes before analysis,
- imply model performance that was not measured,
- present aesthetic storytelling as proof,
- confuse correlation with causality,
- use too many charts without interpretation,
- use too much interpretation without evidence,
- become a generic dashboard,
- become a purely artistic website with weak business value.

## Evidence standards

### Claim types and required support

#### Descriptive claims
Examples:
- top brands,
- frequency patterns,
- basket differences,
- market or axis distributions.
Required support:
- direct descriptive statistics or visual summaries.

#### Comparative claims
Examples:
- one group is more valuable,
- one recommendation logic is stronger than another,
- one brand family creates better return potential.
Required support:
- a comparison baseline,
- a defined metric,
- explanation of why the comparison matters.

#### Predictive claims
Examples:
- customer X is likely to adopt brand Y,
- group A has higher brand adoption potential,
- brand Z is a high-opportunity next brand.
Required support:
- explicit methodology,
- feature rationale,
- validation logic or proxy evaluation,
- limits clearly acknowledged.

#### Strategic claims
Examples:
- Sephora should prioritize this activation,
- this should feed CRM first,
- this brand family is a better focus area.
Required support:
- link between analytical result and business use case,
- explanation of expected value,
- acknowledgment of operational assumptions.

## Storytelling rules

### Rule 1
The final story must emerge from the strongest validated insight.

### Rule 2
If no strong narrative pattern appears in the data, the team must prefer clarity over forced storytelling.

### Rule 3
Any artistic concept chosen later must fit the results.
The concept must adapt to the findings, not the findings to the concept.

### Rule 4
A story arc is allowed only if each section answers a real analytical question.

### Rule 5
If a cinematic or scroll-based format is used, each scene must correspond to one of the following:
- problem,
- method,
- evidence,
- insight,
- recommendation,
- activation,
- business value,
- limitation.

## Visual direction rules

### Visual identity
The final deliverable should feel premium, controlled, and beauty-relevant.

### Allowed qualities
- elegant,
- cinematic,
- minimal,
- sensorial,
- luxury-inspired,
- interactive if useful.

### Forbidden qualities
- noisy,
- gimmicky,
- over-animated,
- childish,
- generic consulting dashboard styling,
- portfolio-style self-promotion.

### Data visualization principle
Charts and graphics must prioritize interpretability first and beauty second.

## Actionability rules

### Every major insight must answer:
- So what?
- For whom?
- Through which channel?
- With what expected benefit?

### Valid activation outputs include:
- CRM targeting,
- app feed personalization,
- e-merchandising ranking,
- store sales guidance,
- brand launch targeting.

### Invalid ending
A finding without a practical activation path should not be treated as a final headline result.

## Final structure template
The final deliverable should eventually follow this logic:

1. Business context
2. Analytical question
3. Data scope and methodology
4. KPI construction and validation logic
5. Core findings
6. Recommendation logic
7. Activation pathways
8. Business value
9. Limits and next steps

This is a structure template only, not a pre-written story.

## Submission checklist
Before finalizing the presentation, confirm that:
- every major claim is supported,
- every visual has a clear reason to exist,
- every important chart is interpreted,
- every recommendation can be activated,
- every conclusion is proportionate to the evidence,
- no section depends on invented results,
- the final experience still feels native to Sephora’s world,
- the output is memorable without becoming unrealistic.

## Final note
The goal is not to create the most artistic presentation.
The goal is to create the most convincing Sephora-relevant interpretation of real findings, expressed with enough creativity to stand out and enough rigor to be trusted.
